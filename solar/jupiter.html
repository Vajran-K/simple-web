Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest planet in our solar system. Known for its massive size, powerful storms, and iconic Great Red Spot, Jupiter plays a key role in the solar system’s dynamics.

Key Facts About Jupiter:

    Distance from the Sun: Approximately 778 million kilometers (484 million miles).
    Diameter: About 142,984 kilometers (88,846 miles), over 11 times the diameter of Earth.
    Day Length: A day on Jupiter lasts only about 10 hours, making it the fastest rotating planet in the solar system.
    Year Length: Jupiter takes about 12 Earth years to complete one orbit around the Sun.

Surface and Atmosphere:

    Surface: Jupiter lacks a solid surface and is composed mostly of hydrogen and helium. Its “surface” is actually a dense, stormy atmosphere.
    Great Red Spot: A massive storm larger than Earth that has been raging for over 300 years.
    Temperature: Average temperature in Jupiter's atmosphere is around -145°C (-234°F).
    Atmosphere: Composed mainly of 90% hydrogen and 10% helium, with traces of methane, ammonia, and water vapor.

Composition and Structure:

    Core: Jupiter may have a small rocky core surrounded by metallic hydrogen.
    Mantle: Made up of dense metallic hydrogen that conducts electricity and generates a powerful magnetic field.
    Outer Layers: Thick layers of gaseous hydrogen and helium form Jupiter's outer layers.
Moons and Rings:

    Moons: Jupiter has at least 95 known moons. The four largest, known as the Galilean Moons, are Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.
    Io: Known for its active volcanoes.
    Europa: Believed to have an ocean beneath its icy crust, making it a prime candidate for potential extraterrestrial life.
    Ganymede: The largest moon in the solar system.
    Callisto: A heavily cratered and ancient moon.
    Rings: Jupiter has a faint ring system made mostly of dust particles from its moons.

Magnetic Field:

    Jupiter has the strongest magnetic field of any planet in the solar system, about 20,000 times stronger than Earth's.
    This magnetic field creates intense radiation belts and powerful auroras at the planet's poles.

Exploration:

    Pioneer 10 & 11: First spacecraft to fly by Jupiter in the early 1970s.
    Voyager 1 & 2: Provided detailed images and data about Jupiter's moons and rings.
    Galileo (1995-2003): Orbited Jupiter and studied its atmosphere and moons.
    Juno (2016-present): Currently studying Jupiter's atmosphere, magnetic field, and internal structure.
    Jupiter's immense size, powerful storms, and fascinating moons make it a cornerstone of planetary science and an exciting target for future space exploration.
