Solar System

The solar system is a vast system consisting of the Sun, eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and interplanetary dust. It formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a giant cloud of gas and dust and is located in the Orion Arm of the Milky Way galaxy.

Key Components:

    The Sun: A massive star at the center, composed mainly of hydrogen and helium. It generates energy through nuclear fusion and accounts for 99.86% of the solar system's mass.

Planets:

    Inner Planets (Terrestrial): Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars are rocky, small, and closer to the Sun.
    Outer Planets (Gas and Ice Giants): Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune are much larger, with thick atmospheres and many moons.
    Dwarf Planets: Objects like Pluto, Ceres, and Eris that orbit the Sun but haven't cleared their orbits of debris.
    Moons: Natural satellites orbiting planets, including Earth's Moon and notable ones like Ganymede (Jupiter) and Titan (Saturn).

Small Bodies:
    Asteroids: Found mainly in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.
    Comets: Icy objects that develop tails when near the Sun.
    Meteoroids: Small rocky fragments, some of which burn up as meteors in Earth's atmosphere.

    Kuiper Belt & Oort Cloud: Distant regions containing icy bodies and the source of many comets.

The solar system is dynamic and interconnected, with gravity and cosmic forces shaping its components. Earth uniquely supports life due to its liquid water and protective atmosphere.
